VGGT-HPE: Reframing Head Pose Estimation as Relative Pose Prediction

Vasiliki Vasileiou1,2,4, Panagiotis P. Filntisis2,3, Petros Maragos2,3,4, Kostas Daniilidis1,5

Abstract

Monocular head pose estimation is traditionally formulated as direct regression from a single image to an absolute pose. This paradigm forces the network to implicitly internalize a dataset-specific canonical reference frame. In this work, we argue that predicting the relative rigid transformation between two observed head configurations is a fundamentally easier and more robust formulation. We introduce VGGT-HPE, a relative head pose estimator built upon a general-purpose geometry foundation model. Fine-tuned exclusively on synthetic facial renderings, our method sidesteps the need for an implicit anchor by reducing the problem to estimating a geometric displacement from an explicitly provided anchor with a known pose. As a practical benefit, the relative formulation also allows the anchor to be chosen at test time so that the prediction difficulty can be controlled by the application. Despite zero real-world training data, VGGT-HPE achieves state-of-the-art results on the BIWI benchmark, outperforming established absolute regression methods trained on mixed and real datasets.

Method

VGGT-HPE takes an anchor image with known pose and a query image, then predicts the relative rigid transformation between them. The method builds on a frozen pre-trained VGGT backbone and adapts it to the facial domain through lightweight LoRA fine-tuning. At test time, the predicted relative transformation is composed with the known anchor pose to recover the absolute pose of the query in the desired coordinate frame.

The relative formulation changes the problem in two important ways. First, it removes the need for an implicit canonical frame learned from the training distribution, because the model estimates a displacement between two visible head configurations. Second, it turns the task into a correspondence and matching problem between an anchor and a query, which transfers better across domains than absolute single-image regression.

  1. We retain only the camera branch of VGGT and disable the remaining heads, keeping the geometric priors learned during large-scale multi-view pre-training.
  2. We adapt the backbone with LoRA in the attention and MLP projections of both frame-level and global transformer blocks, using rank 8 and scaling factor 16.
  3. Training uses 25k synthetic FLAME renderings from 250 identities, with paired appearance augmentation and supervision on translation, rotation, and field-of-view so that rotation accuracy benefits from full camera-parameter learning.

Results

VGGT-HPE achieves state-of-the-art performance on BIWI while training only on synthetic facial renderings. Under the shared MTCNN evaluation protocol, the relative model reaches 2.24 degrees yaw error, 3.04 degrees pitch error, and 2.82 degrees mean absolute error, while the absolute single-image variant built on the same VGGT backbone reaches 5.15 degrees MAE. This makes the benefit structural rather than architectural: the performance gain comes from the relative formulation itself.

Each row shows a different BIWI subject. From left to right: the query frame, the anchor frame with its known pose overlay, the ground-truth pose, our prediction, and three baselines. The relative model stays visually closer to the ground truth across near-profile and upward-looking cases where absolute methods deviate more strongly.

When the anchor is near-neutral and the anchor-query rotation gap increases, VGGT-HPE remains the strongest method across the full range. The gap to absolute baselines widens as the target pose moves farther away from the implicit frontal reference they internalize during training.

When each query is paired with a same-subject anchor whose anchor-query gap is below 5 degrees, VGGT-HPE stays below 2 degrees MAE across the full absolute pose range. With a close enough anchor, extreme poses become no harder than frontal ones.
